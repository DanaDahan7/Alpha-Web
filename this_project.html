Performance Study for ALFA-Mix

In this academic project, I've closely examined Alpha-Mix, a machine learning method. The aim was to assess how well Alpha-Mix performs under various conditions. I explored different scenarios by altering the amount of labeled data, using 10,000 and 50,000 labeled samples, and tried it with various deep learning models like MLP, ResNet, and Vision-Transformers. Diverse datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR100, and CIFAR10, were also part of the study.

In order to test the performance of Alpha-Mix, I implemented the model based on the article with the help of the article's repository on GitHub. First I encountered a number of bugs from the Alpha-Mix repository, I fixed the bugs and created a fork that I used throughout the project, My fork for Alpha Mix.

Throughout the project, I conducted a series of tests on Alpha-Mix using different datasets and models. The objective was to determine when Alpha-Mix outperforms traditional methods and when there's little difference compared to randomly selecting data.

Here's the key distinction: Alpha-Mix intelligently selects which data to label by considering both labeled and unlabeled samples, looking for inconsistencies in predictions. In contrast, random sampling adds data without any specific rules. I updated the labeled data in both cases, but with Alpha-Mix, I followed a specific method for data selection.

After each update, such as training the model with 10% of the data and then with 20% of the data, I measured the model's performance. This allowed me to compare Alpha-Mix with random sampling and understand when Alpha-Mix proves more effective. I conducted these analyses using different models, datasets, and various amounts of training data to gain a comprehensive understanding of Alpha-Mix's strengths and limitations.
