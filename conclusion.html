Conclusions

  • In the case of MNIST, Alpha-Mix outperformed random sampling.

  • In all other tests conducted on CIFAR10 and CIFAR100, there is no significant improvement observed between Alpha-Mix and random sampling. Even when Alpha-Mix does show an advantage, it's typically by a margin of at most 3% in terms of accuracy.

  • When employing a higher number of epochs in a low-data regime, there is no discernible improvement between Alpha-Mix and random sampling. In other words, as the number of epochs increases, both sample methods exhibit relatively similar performance improvements.

  • The Vision Transformers model consistently yields poor performance on the CIFAR100 dataset, irrespective of whether Alpha-Mix or random sampling is employed.

  • Alpha-Mix appears to have potential, as indicated by graphs in the referenced article. However, I encountered challenges in replicating these results, possibly due to limitations in computational resources.

Suggestions for further research

  • Evaluate Alpha-Mix with increased computational resources to assess its potential for improved performance.

  • Extend the application of Alpha-Mix to diverse datasets, including non-image data like video, to gain a deeper understanding of its capabilities.

  • Investigate the performance of Alpha-Mix on datasets containing partial mislabeling to determine whether it can enhance accuracy in such scenarios.
